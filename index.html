The Invisible Layers

From Ozone Recovery to Carbon Emissions

This project contrasts two atmospheric narratives — how global cooperation through the Montreal Protocol (1987) successfully healed the ozone layer, and how unregulated data center expansion and AI infrastructure are driving new carbon emissions. Together, they reveal how policy can make—or break—the planet’s future climate.

The Montreal Protocol: A Timeline of Global Action

The Montreal Protocol, signed in 1987, stands as one of humanity’s greatest environmental successes. It emerged after decades of scientific warnings about chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs), chemicals used in refrigeration and aerosols that were found to destroy the ozone layer. Through global cooperation, nations phased out these substances, leading to the gradual healing of the ozone layer — a rare proof that science, policy, and collective action can reverse planetary damage.

The Protocol continues to evolve, adjusting to new findings and technologies. Subsequent amendments, such as the London and Kigali Amendments, expanded its reach to include additional substances like hydrochlorofluorocarbons (HCFCs) and hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs). Its success shows how international regulation can translate data into environmental recovery — a model for addressing today’s unregulated carbon emissions.

1974

Mario Molina and F. Sherwood Rowland discover that CFCs destroy ozone molecules, marking the beginning of modern atmospheric chemistry awareness.

1985

Vienna Convention for the Protection of the Ozone Layer is adopted, establishing a scientific and legal framework for international cooperation.

1987

The Montreal Protocol is signed by 46 countries, committing to phase out ozone-depleting substances like CFCs and halons.

1990

London Amendments strengthen phaseout deadlines and provide funding for developing nations to transition away from harmful chemicals.

2016

Kigali Amendment expands the Protocol to include HFCs, linking ozone recovery with climate action by targeting greenhouse gases.

2024

NASA and WMO confirm record ozone recovery over Antarctica, projecting near-complete restoration of pre-1980 ozone levels by mid-century.

Ozone Recovery After the Montreal Protocol

The Montreal Protocol (1987) stands as one of the most successful international environmental agreements in history. It banned ozone‑depleting substances such as CFCs and halons, leading to a measurable recovery of the ozone layer in just a few decades. Using NASA satellite data (MOD, OMI), we visualize this long‑term improvement in global ozone concentration from 1979 to today.

Global Total Column Ozone — 1979–2024

Global total column ozone (DU)

About this data: The graph shows the global mean total column ozone measured in Dobson Units (DU). One DU represents a 0.01 mm thick layer of pure ozone at standard conditions. Higher DU values mean stronger UV protection, while lower DU indicates depletion. The decline in the 1980s–1990s corresponds to widespread CFC use, while the recovery since 2000 reflects the effects of the Montreal Protocol.

The Rise of Data Centers and CO₂

The digital revolution has brought an invisible footprint. Data centers — the physical engines behind AI and cloud computing — now account for a rapidly increasing share of global energy use. Yet unlike ozone‑depleting substances, these infrastructures remain largely unregulated, consuming vast amounts of electricity and water, and producing CO₂ emissions concentrated in specific regions across the U.S.

Regulated vs Unregulated

The Montreal Protocol shows that collective regulation works — over 190 nations agreed to phase out ozone‑depleting chemicals, and the results are visible in the atmosphere today. In contrast, carbon emissions from digital infrastructure continue to rise without consistent global or national policy frameworks. Through our visualization, we compare how policy enforcement versus policy absence shapes the trajectory of environmental recovery.
